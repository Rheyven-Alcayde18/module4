What is Usability Evaluation?

Usability evaluation methods contribute to systems development by providing feedback on usability problems and validating the usability of a system. There are four main reasons for doing evaluation:

  • To understand the real world and improve designs to fit the workplace better.
  • To compare designs — determine which is best for given situations and users.
  • To decide if engineering targets have been met — is the design good enough?
  • To decide if the design meets standards.

Types of Evaluation

Formative

During Development

Conducted during the development process to identify and fix usability problems early. Uses prototypes, simulations, or early versions. Main goal: refine the system and reduce the risk of expensive rework before the final product is completed.

Summative

After Development

Conducted at a later stage or after development. Measures whether the product meets usability requirements, user satisfaction, and performance standards. Determines if the system is ready for release.

Method 1 — Testing

🧪
Testing
Representative users work on typical tasks; evaluators assess how the UI supports users.
1.1
Coaching Method
Participants are allowed to ask any system-related questions of an expert coach who answers to the best of their ability during the usability test.
1.2
Co-discovery Learning
Two test users attempt to perform tasks together while being observed, helping each other as they would in a real collaborative work situation.
1.3
Performance Measurement
Obtains quantitative data about participants' performance. Conducted in a formal usability laboratory to collect data accurately and minimize unexpected interference.
1.4
Question Asking Protocol
Also obtains quantitative performance data during usability testing. Should be conducted in a formal usability laboratory for accurate data collection and minimal interference.
1.5
Remote Testing
Used when testers are separated in space and/or time from participants. Testers cannot observe the testing process directly; participants are not usually in a formal usability lab.

Method 2 — Inspection

🔍
Inspection
Usability specialists (and sometimes developers/users) examine usability-related aspects of a UI.
2.1
Cognitive Walkthrough
One or more evaluators inspect a UI by going through a set of tasks and evaluate its understandability and ease of learning. Can use paper mockups, prototypes, or fully developed interfaces.
2.2
Feature Inspection
Focuses on the feature set of a product. Inspectors analyze each feature for its availability, understandability, and other usability aspects using given use cases.
2.3
Heuristic Evaluation
Developed by Jakob Nielsen and Rolf Molich. Uses a set of heuristics (guidelines/rules of thumb) to structure the critique of a system. Identifies usability problems against established principles.
2.4
Pluralistic Walkthrough
Group walkthrough that provides a diverse range of skills and perspectives. More people looking for problems = higher probability of finding problems.

Method 3 — Inquiry

💬
Inquiry
Evaluators gather info about users' likes, dislikes, needs, and understanding by talking to or observing them.
3.1
Field Observation
Human factors engineers go to a representative user's workplace and observe them working, to understand how they use the system and what mental model they have. Used in test and deployment stages.
3.2
Focus Group
About 6 to 9 users are brought together to discuss issues relating to the system. A human factors engineer acts as moderator, preparing discussion topics in advance.
3.3
Interviews
Human factors engineers formulate questions and interview representative users. Two types: Unstructured Interview (open-ended, flexible) and Structured Interview (fixed questions, consistent format).
3.4
Questionnaires
Focus on sampling a statistically representative smaller population. More manageable, reduces workload and costs, and makes it easier to ensure data homogeneity and quality.
3.5
Logging Actual Users
The computer automatically collects statistics about detailed system use. Useful because it shows how users perform real work and easily collects data from a large number of users under different circumstances.

8 Factors for Choosing an Evaluation Technique

01
Stage in the Cycle
The stage in the design process at which evaluation is required affects the choice of method.
02
Style of Evaluation
Both laboratory studies (early stages) and field studies (new implementation) should ideally be included.
03
Subjectivity vs. Objectivity
Subjective techniques (e.g., cognitive walkthrough) rely on the evaluator's knowledge and expertise.
04
Type of Measures
Quantitative (numeric, statistically analyzed) vs. qualitative measurement — both serve different purposes.
05
Information Provided
The level of information needed may range from low-level details to enable a design decision to be made.
06
Immediacy of Response
Some methods record behavior at the time of interaction (e.g., think aloud), but this can alter how the user works.
07
Level of Interference
Intrusive techniques that produce immediate measurements risk influencing the user's behavior during the interaction.
08
Resources Required
Consider equipment, time, money, participants, evaluator expertise, and context. Resource limitations can force certain choices.
